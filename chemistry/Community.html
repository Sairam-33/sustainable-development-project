COMMUNITY NETWORKS

Community networks are decentralized networks that provide internet access and digital services to local communities, typically managed and operated by members of the community. These networks are created in areas where traditional internet service providers (ISPs) may not reach or where connectivity is limited or unaffordable. By leveraging local infrastructure and community resources, they help bridge the digital divide, making technology more accessible and empowering communities to improve their connectivity on their own terms.

Key Aspects of Community Networks:

Decentralization and Local Control

Community networks are owned and managed locally, which promotes self-reliance. Local residents often make key decisions about the network, including coverage, services, and policies. This local governance approach enables communities to focus on their specific needs and priorities, enhancing social inclusion and digital access.

Affordable and Sustainable Connectivity

Since community networks are typically nonprofit, the primary goal is to provide affordable access rather than maximize profits. They are often funded by donations, grants, or small fees, making them a sustainable and viable solution for underserved regions. This affordability makes them particularly appealing in rural areas or low-income communities.

Capacity Building and Digital Skills Development

Community networks promote digital literacy and technical skills development among community members. By participating in network setup, maintenance, and operations, locals gain valuable IT and management skills. This knowledge transfer can lead to broader socio-economic benefits as residents become more capable of managing digital resources and using online platforms for various community services.

Promoting Social and Economic Development

Reliable internet access can facilitate economic growth and community welfare. Community networks can support education, healthcare, agriculture, and business in underserved areas. Through internet access, students gain access to online resources, farmers can access market prices, and health workers can reach training and resources.

Challenges and Limitations

While community networks have many benefits, they face challenges like limited funding, regulatory barriers, technical expertise, and equipment availability. Local infrastructure setup and maintenance can be difficult without sustained financial and technical support. Additionally, these networks may have limited bandwidth compared to commercial ISPs, which may restrict their service quality.

Examples of Community Networks

Guifi.net in Spain:

A large-scale, successful community network that has helped bring high-speed internet to remote areas of Catalonia and beyond.

Zenzeleni in South Africa:

Provides affordable internet in rural communities, supporting educational and healthcare services.

NYC Mesh in the United States:

A mesh network offering low-cost internet access to underserved neighborhoods in New York City.

Impact of Community Networks on Sustainable Development

Community networks align with sustainable development goals (SDGs) by fostering inclusive, equitable internet access, and by reducing inequalities in technology access. They empower communities to connect, communicate, and grow economically and socially.
